ON EXILE

The throat knows before the mind does. Something tightens there, at the
back, where the soft palate meets the passage to the lungs, and it
tightens not because of what you see but because of what you no longer
see. A street. A particular arrangement of light on plaster at four in
the afternoon. The sound a door makes in a building where the stairwell
amplifies everything — footsteps, arguments, the dragging of furniture
across a floor you will never stand on again. The throat closes around
these absences the way a hand closes around nothing.

You carry the old country in your spine. Not metaphorically. Your
vertebrae remember the particular hunch against cold that your
grandfather had, that your mother had, that you acquired without
instruction. In the new place, where the air smells of jasmine and
exhaust and something sweet rotting behind a shawarma stand, your spine
still braces against a winter that exists only in the nervous system.
The body does not update its geography. The body is a country that
refuses to redraw its borders.

Brodsky wrote that language is the only homeland a poet has. He was lying
in the way brilliant men lie — by saying something so beautiful it
becomes true before anyone checks. Language is not a homeland. Language
is the phantom limb. You reach for a word in the old tongue and
something fires in the chest — warmth, tenderness, recognition — and
then the word arrives and it fits in no mouth around you. You say тоска
and the person across the table hears a sound, not the weight of
longing-without-object that the word carries, that heaviness in the
sternum like a stone. You say געגועים and even the Israelis do not know
YOUR געגועים, the one that tastes of snow and kerosene and the silence
of a Soviet kitchen at night when the radio has been turned off and the
only sound is the refrigerator and your father breathing.

Exile is not dramatic. It is not the moment of departure — the airport,
the last embrace, the looking back. Those are events. They have edges.
Exile is what comes after. Exile is Tuesday. Exile is standing in a
supermarket holding a carton of milk and suddenly your hands feel like
they belong to someone else. The fluorescent lights buzz at a frequency
subtly different from the fluorescent lights of your childhood, and this
difference enters the body through the ears and settles in the stomach.
Not nausea. Something worse. A hollowness that has weight.

Emptiness, when it becomes chronic, develops texture. Not the dramatic
void — not the abyss of poets. More like a fabric. A thin, grey, damp
fabric lining the inside of the chest. You forget it is there. For
months you forget. You laugh. You cook dinner. You argue about politics.
And then something — the angle of light, a smell, a song through a
wall — presses against that lining and you feel the whole weight of the
forgotten, the abandoned architecture of a life you left behind, and
your ribs tighten and your breath catches and for three seconds you are
nowhere. Numb. Paralyzed between two versions of yourself, neither
complete.

The fear is not of forgetting. The fear is of having already forgotten.
You test yourself. You try to remember the address. Ulitsa... what was
it? The building was yellow. Or was it beige? There was a courtyard. You
played there. But the courtyard expands and contracts in memory like
something breathing, and you are no longer sure if the tree was a birch
or a linden, and this uncertainty — this tiny, specific uncertainty about
a tree that you climbed when you were seven — this is the dread. Not
existential. Dermatological. It is on the surface of the skin, this
dread, it is in the fingertips, a tingling, an itch that cannot be
scratched because the thing that itches no longer exists.

Le mal du pays. The body, deprived of its native coordinates, consumes
itself. Not violently. Gently. From the inside, invisibly, until what
remains is a shape that resembles the original but is hollow.

You learn to live in the hollow. You furnish it. You hang curtains in
the emptiness. You make tea while looking out a window at a landscape
that will never be yours and you feel — not sadness — but a kind of
tenderness for the glass that separates you from a world that did not
ask for your presence. Warmth, even. A strange warmth that comes not
from belonging but from witnessing. The spine straightens. The hands
steady. The throat loosens by a fraction.

My mother kept a jar of black earth from Odessa behind the spices. I did
not know this until she died. The earth was dry and grey by then — twenty
years in glass will do that — but when I unscrewed the lid there was
something, a ghost of wet soil and autumn leaves, something I cannot
name, that entered through the nose and landed in the chest and sat
there for days. Not grief. Grief has edges. This was the body
recognizing a place it had lost. The body saying: I was built from this.

The exile does not end. There is no scene in which you return and the
circle closes. There is instead a slow, geological process in which the
new place deposits its sediment over the old. Layer upon layer. Забыто,
forgotten, oublie, but not gone. Never gone. It lives in the posture. In
the way the hands move when the mouth is speaking a language that is not
the first. In the way the shoulders rise toward the ears when a siren
sounds, because in the old place sirens meant something different,
something that made the whole body clench and the eyes search for
shelter.

You do not choose to carry this. The body chooses. The body, which has
no politics and no passport and no opinion about borders, carries
everything. It carries the embrace of a grandmother whose name you
mispronounce now. It carries the fear of a night you were too young to
understand. It carries the silence of rooms where difficult things were
not said, where silence itself was a language, where the absence of
words had the weight of words, and this silence — this inherited,
transported, replicated silence — settles into the void between the ribs
and stays.

Exile is the body's longest memory. It outlasts language. It outlasts
love. It outlasts the will to remember. When everything else has been
forgotten — the street names, the faces, the taste of the water — the
body still flinches at the wrong sound, still reaches for a door handle
on the wrong side, still wakes at three in the morning with the chest
tight and the hands cold and the unmistakable feeling that it is
somewhere else, somewhere that no longer exists, somewhere it will spend
the rest of its life trying to return to, not by moving, but by
breathing, slowly, in the dark, in the abandoned space between
heartbeats.
